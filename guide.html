01

The Science of Interviewing

85 years of research shows structured interviews are the single strongest predictor of job performance.

📊

Structured vs. Unstructured

The landmark Schmidt & Hunter (1998) meta-analysis found structured interviews have a predictive validity of .51 for job performance, versus .38 for unstructured interviews. The updated Sackett et al. (2022) study confirmed structured interviews are now ranked #1 across all selection methods, with more than double the predictive power and nearly a third less bias than unstructured interviews.

✅

What Makes an Interview Structured?

💡

Google's Finding

Google's internal research confirmed that structured interview scores are "highly predictive indicators of future performance scores" and that both candidates and interviewers report a better experience and perceive the process as more fair.

The Four Pillars of a Scoring Rubric

🧠

Problem Solving

Organized approach, considers multiple solutions, evaluates tradeoffs

💻

Technical Skill

Clean, correct code; language mastery; efficient implementation

💬

Communication

Explains approach clearly, asks clarifying questions, thinks out loud

🧪

Testing

Tests against normal and edge cases, self-corrects issues proactively

Psychology of Interviewing

Your brain is working against you. Understanding cognitive biases is the first step to mitigating them.

Cognitive Biases That Affect Interviewers

Click each bias to learn what it is and how to counter it.

What it is: One positive attribute (prestigious employer, eloquent speech) creates a "glow" that inflates evaluation of everything else.

Mitigation: Evaluate each competency separately using a structured rubric with behavioral anchors. Score question-by-question, not holistically.

What it is: Within the first 30 seconds, you form an initial impression, then spend the rest of the interview seeking confirming information.

Mitigation: Write your rating for question one before hearing the answer to question two. Evaluate evidence in isolation.

What it is: Once you form an early impression, you unconsciously steer questions to validate it — asking about successes from strong-seeming candidates and challenges from weaker-seeming ones.

Mitigation: Use identical questions for all candidates. Practice "devil's advocate" thinking — hypothesize what evidence might disprove your initial take.

What it is: The tendency to rate more favorably people who remind us of ourselves — same school, hobbies, cultural references.

Mitigation: Diverse interview panels. Blinded reviews where possible. Conscious awareness training.

What it is: Opposite of halo — a single negative trait (quiet introduction, accent, resume gap) creates a negative impression of the entire candidate.

Mitigation: Structured rubrics, competency-by-competency evaluation. Record specific examples, not vague impressions.

What it is: Your perception of a candidate is influenced by the previous one. A good candidate after several poor ones appears exceptional; a good candidate after an excellent one seems mediocre.

Mitigation: Evaluate each candidate against the rubric, never against other candidates. Take breaks between interviews to reset.

What it is: Snap judgments made in the first few minutes that color the entire interview. Research shows most interviewers make a decision within 4 minutes.

Mitigation: Delay forming any overall judgment until after reviewing all notes. Structured scoring forces evaluation based on answers to specific questions.

Partner Mindset

  • Collaborative — "Let's solve this together"
  • Creates psychological safety
  • Reveals true ability
  • Better candidate experience
  • Integrative: mutual benefit

Interrogator Mindset

  • Adversarial — "Prove yourself to me"
  • Creates anxiety & stress
  • Masks true ability
  • Damages employer brand
  • Distributive: zero-sum game
💬
Research from Cambridge found that candidates who completed at least 3 mock interviews improved coding accuracy by 27% and reported a 40% reduction in anxiety. Stress significantly impacts technical interview performance — your job as an interviewer is to minimize it.

04

Opening the Interview

The first 5 minutes set the tone. A candidate who feels safe will perform closer to their actual ability.

Do

  • Greet warmly and introduce yourself (name, role, team)
  • Explain the interview format, timing, and what to expect
  • Brief small talk about how they found the role
  • Tell them it's okay to ask questions and take time to think
  • Say: "I'm interested in how you think, not just the answer"

Don't

  • Start with pressure questions or brainteasers
  • Ask abstract icebreakers ("If you were an appetizer...")
  • Skip warm-up and jump into technical questions
  • Rush through introductions or appear distracted
  • Check your phone or multitask

Great Icebreakers

"What are you working on these days that you find interesting?"
"Have you had a chance to look at our product?"
"How did you hear about this position?"
"I noticed you worked on [X] — that sounds really cool."
🎯

Setting Expectations Template

"We have about 45 minutes. I'll spend the first few minutes getting to know you, then we'll work through a technical problem together, and I'll leave time at the end for your questions. I'm interested in seeing how you think through problems — feel free to think out loud, ask questions, and use whatever approach feels natural."
05

Question Design

The quality of your questions determines the quality of your signal. Design questions that reveal depth, not just recall.

The STAR Method for Behavioral Questions

S Situation Context so you understand the setting ~20% of answer
T Task The specific responsibility or challenge ~10% of answer
A Action What they personally did — the core of the answer ~60% of answer
R Result Measurable outcomes and what they learned ~10% of answer

Probing Follow-up Questions

"What would you do differently if you faced this again?"
"What was the hardest part?"
"Can you quantify the impact?"
"How did that change your approach going forward?"
"What did you learn from that experience?"

Green Flags

  • Specific examples with concrete details
  • Asks clarifying questions before diving in
  • Discusses tradeoffs unprompted
  • Considers edge cases proactively
  • Admits what they don't know
  • Shows genuine curiosity and growth mindset

Red Flags

  • Vague answers suggesting borrowed/memorized code
  • Can't explain their own past projects
  • Blames others without showing what they learned
  • Evasive — provides philosophies instead of implementations
  • Off-topic rambling to avoid answering
  • Only talks about individual work (for senior+ roles)

Live Coding Best Practices

1

Explain the format: "I want to see how you think, not just the final answer"

2

Let the candidate choose their programming language

3

Have 2-3 prepared solutions in different languages with time/space complexities noted

4

Silence is okay — let them think; don't fill every pause with hints

5

Evaluate approach, communication, and response to being stuck — not just correctness

6

Structure: 5-10 min intro, 30-40 min coding, 10-15 min discussion

06

Active Listening & Observation

The best interviewers listen 80% and talk 20%. What you observe is as important as what you hear.

The 80/20 Rule

Candidate speaks 80%
You 20%

If you're talking more than 20% of the time, you're talking too much.

What to Observe Beyond the Verbal Answer

🧩

Problem-solving approach

Do they decompose? Plan before coding? Consider edge cases?

💬

Communication

Can they explain their reasoning clearly? Do they think out loud?

💪

Reaction to difficulty

Frustration, persistence, adaptability, willingness to ask for help

🎓

Coachability

How they respond to hints and feedback — do they incorporate it?

Note-Taking Best Practices

Write

  • Key answers and specific examples
  • Problem-solving approach and reasoning
  • Notable quotes (direct words)
  • Technical accuracy of answers

Don't Write

  • Impressions about protected characteristics
  • Vague feelings ("seemed off")
  • Physical appearance descriptions
  • Personal opinions unrelated to job skills
⏰

Finalize your notes within 1 hour of the interview while memory is fresh.

07

Hints & Managing Difficulty

The art of helping without giving away the answer. Find the Goldilocks zone of challenge.

Hint Calibration Framework

Level 1 Small Nudge

Try this first. Minimal guidance that points in a direction.

"What data structure might help with lookups?"
"Have you considered the edge case where the input is empty?"
Level 2 Direction Change

If the small nudge didn't help. More explicit guidance.

"Let's step back. What if we used a hash map?"
"Can you think of a way to avoid the nested loop?"
Level 3 Major Guidance

Last resort. Walk through a small example together.

"The key insight here is [concept]. How would you apply that?"
🎯

The Goldilocks Zone

Too Easy

No signal. Everyone passes. Can't differentiate.

Just Right

Candidates demonstrate thinking. Good ones solve it. Struggling ones show approach.

Too Hard

No signal. Everyone fails. Only tests luck.

When a Candidate is Completely Stuck

1

Ask open-ended: "What approaches have you considered?"

2

Ask them to think out loud about what they DO know

3

Give incremental hints through conversation

4

Don't let them sit in silence for extended periods

5

If no progress after multiple hints, move to a different problem

08

Evaluation & Scoring

Precise scoring prevents "everyone gets a 3" syndrome. Define what each score means with concrete behaviors.

Rating Scale with Behavioral Anchors

1
Unsatisfactory

Answer missed the point entirely. Cannot demonstrate basic competency. Significant gaps in understanding.

2
Below Expectations

Poor or incomplete answer with some good points. Needs significant guidance to make progress. Fundamental misunderstandings present.

3
Meets Expectations

Adequate answer hitting key points. Competent but not standout. Solved the problem with minor issues. Communicated reasoning.

4
Exceeds Expectations

Strong answer going beyond requirements. Shows depth, nuance, and awareness of tradeoffs. Clean implementation with good edge case handling.

5
Exceptional

Excellent answer demonstrating mastery. Optimal solution with clear explanation of alternatives. Proactively discussed testing, monitoring, and production concerns.

💡
Medium's engineering team insight: "We can teach someone who is lacking in one or two technical categories, provided they have the right attitude. It is harder to change a person's personality, or gain alignment with them on values."
09

Closing the Interview

How you end matters. Leave every candidate with a positive impression of you and your team.

Say

  • "Thank you for your time today"
  • "I enjoyed our discussion about [topic]"
  • "Our recruiting team will follow up within [timeframe]"
  • "Feel free to reach out if you have questions"

Don't Say

  • "You did great!" (creates false expectations)
  • "I don't think this is a fit" (premature)
  • "We'll let you know" without a timeframe
  • Anything about salary, offer likelihood, or other candidates

Time Management

1

Keep a clock visible throughout the interview

2

Give a verbal signal: "We have about 5 minutes left, so let me move to your questions"

3

Never run over — it's disrespectful to everyone's schedule

4

If you couldn't cover everything, note what was and wasn't assessed

10

Post-Interview

Your feedback shapes hiring decisions. Write it while the memory is fresh, and make it evidence-based.

Writing Effective Feedback

Debrief Rules

1

All feedback must be submitted independently before the debrief meeting

2

Let less-senior people speak first (avoids echoing the boss)

3

Require evidence for every assessment — no "gut feelings"

4

Distinguish between "did not demonstrate" and "demonstrated poorly"

5

Assign a devil's advocate to argue against the consensus

11

Common Mistakes

Even experienced interviewers fall into these traps. Awareness is the first step to avoidance.

01

Talking too much

If you're speaking more than 20% of the time, the candidate can't show you what they know.

02

Being unprepared

Not reviewing the resume or knowing the role requirements. Winging it with ad-hoc questions.

03

Succumbing to bias

Halo effect, similarity bias, confirmation bias — without structured rubrics, bias runs the show.

04

Snap judgments

Forming opinions in the first 30 seconds and spending the rest confirming them.

05

Testing memorization

Asking trivia instead of problem-solving questions that reveal depth of understanding.

06

Asking generic questions

"Tell me about yourself" without follow-up. Questions unrelated to job competencies.

07

Inconsistent process

Different questions, formats, or criteria for different candidates makes comparison impossible.

08

Selling too hard

Spending the interview marketing the company instead of assessing the candidate.

09

No candidate Q&A

Cutting off their questions signals disrespect and misses a chance to evaluate curiosity.

10

Interrupting

Breaking their train of thought, especially during technical problems. Let them finish.

11

Vague scoring

Rubrics that produce "everyone gets a 3" — useless for making hiring decisions.

12

No follow-up

Leaving candidates in the dark about next steps. This damages your employer brand permanently.

Interviewing by Level

What you assess — and what you expect — changes dramatically by seniority level.

0 yearsLearning tasks

Focus Areas

Fundamentals, curiosity, learning speed, enthusiasm

What to Expect

  • Basic data structures and algorithms
  • Willingness to learn and ask questions
  • Clear communication of simple concepts

Don't Expect

  • Production experience
  • System design or architectural decisions
  • Framework-level knowledge

Green Flags

Asks great questions, eager to learn, can implement basic solutions with guidance, shows genuine curiosity

1-3 yearsOwn tasks

Focus Areas

Core competency in 1-2 languages/frameworks, basic problem-solving

What to Expect

  • Solid fundamentals and clean functional code
  • Basic debugging skills
  • Understanding of common patterns

Don't Expect

  • Deep system design
  • Mentoring ability
  • Architectural vision

Key Insight

Over-engineering is worse than under-engineering at this level. Look for simplicity and correctness.

3-5 yearsFeature-level

Focus Areas

Mastery of 2-3 frameworks, independent feature delivery, handling moderate ambiguity

What to Expect

  • Standard patterns known well
  • Ability to design features end-to-end
  • Writes tests and considers edge cases

Assessment Focus

Can they work independently? Do they think beyond the happy path? Can they explain tradeoffs in their past decisions?

5+ yearsProject/team level

Focus Areas

Architectural decisions, mentoring, tradeoff analysis, leading complex projects

What to Expect

  • Deep expertise and proactive tradeoff discussion
  • Drives solutions through ambiguity independently
  • In system design: discusses actual numbers (QPS, storage, latency)
  • References real-world systems and past experience

Key Differentiator

Must demonstrate influence beyond their own code. Look for mentoring, technical leadership, and cross-team impact.

7+ yearsOrg/multi-team

Focus Areas

Strategic technical direction, cross-team influence, organizational impact

What to Expect

  • Sets vision and guides technical strategy
  • Drives the entire system design conversation
  • Identifies requirements the interviewer didn't mention
  • Proposes monitoring, alerting, and operational concerns

Key Differentiator

Resolves disagreements between teams, not just within. Considers technical details in context of the bigger organizational picture.

13

Legal & Ethics

There are questions you must never ask. Period. Protect the candidate and your organization.

Never Ask About

🚫Age

Retirement plans, graduation dates, birth year

🚫Family Status

Spouse, children, childcare, pregnancy, family planning

🚫Religion

Religious practices, church, holidays observed

🚫Disability

Past illnesses, prescriptions, physical/mental health

🚫National Origin

Birthplace, ancestry, accent, native language

🚫Gender / Orientation

Gender identity, sexual orientation, relationship status

🚫Race / Ethnicity

Racial background, ethnic heritage, genetic info

🚫Criminal History

Many jurisdictions have "Ban the Box" laws

You CAN Ask

  • "Are you authorized to work in [country]?"
  • "Can you perform the essential functions of this role with or without reasonable accommodation?"
  • "Are you available to work [schedule]?"
  • Questions directly related to job qualifications and competencies
