Interview Guide

How to conduct effective, fair, and structured technical interviews

Contents

1. Before the Interview 2. Interview Structure 3. Introduction Phase 4. Technical Questions 5. Live Coding 6. Scoring & Rating 7. Avoiding Bias 8. Wrap-up & Next Steps 9. Do's & Don'ts

1. Before the Interview

Review the candidate's resume and note specific areas to explore
Select topics relevant to the role (not just your personal expertise)
Prepare the interview plan (adjust phases and timing)
Test your setup: timer, questions loaded, quiet room
Review the scoring rubric so ratings are consistent
Clear 10-15 extra minutes for notes after the interview
üí°Tip

Customize the topic selection based on the role. A junior iOS developer doesn't need System Design questions, and a senior architect should get fewer basics.

2. Interview Structure

A well-structured interview has distinct phases. The default plan is optimized for 60-minute interviews:

Introduction (5 min) ‚Äî Build rapport, set expectations
Theory (25 min) ‚Äî Conceptual questions, adaptive difficulty
Code Questions (10 min) ‚Äî Code reading, output prediction
Live Coding (15 min) ‚Äî Problem solving, communication
Wrap-up (5 min) ‚Äî Candidate questions, next steps
üí°Tip

You can reorder and adjust phase durations in the Interview Plan section. For shorter interviews, consider dropping one phase entirely rather than rushing all of them.

3. Introduction Phase

The first few minutes set the tone. A nervous candidate won't perform their best.

You: "Hi [name], thanks for taking the time. I'm [your name], [your role]. Before we start, let me explain how this will work..."
You: "We'll start with some conversation about your background, then move into technical questions and a coding exercise. Feel free to think aloud ‚Äî I'm interested in your thought process, not just the answer."
You: "There are no trick questions. If something is unclear, just ask. Ready?"
‚ö†Ô∏èWatch out

Don't skip the introduction to "save time." A 2-minute warm-up can improve the quality of the entire remaining interview.

4. Technical Questions

The tool automatically adjusts difficulty based on previous answers. Your job is to:

Read the question clearly ‚Äî don't paraphrase or add hints
Give the candidate time to think (10-15 seconds of silence is OK)
Use the hint button if they're stuck ‚Äî note whether it helped
Ask follow-up questions: "Can you elaborate?" or "What would happen if...?"
Rate immediately after each question while it's fresh
Skip questions that don't fit ‚Äî it's OK to skip
üí°Tip

If a candidate gives a partial answer, probe deeper: "You mentioned X ‚Äî can you explain why that matters?" This helps distinguish 3-star from 4-star answers.

5. Live Coding

Live coding tests problem-solving, communication, and practical ability. It's not about writing perfect code.

Encourage thinking aloud ‚Äî silence during coding is a red flag
Offer hints progressively if they're stuck (use the hint buttons)
Note how they respond to hints ‚Äî do they course-correct quickly?
Ask about edge cases: "What if the input is empty?"
Ask about time/space complexity after they finish
It's OK if they don't finish ‚Äî process matters more than completion
‚ö†Ô∏èWatch out

Never let a candidate struggle in silence for more than 2 minutes. Either offer a hint or move on. Prolonged silence creates anxiety and doesn't provide useful signal.

6. Scoring & Rating

Consistent scoring across interviews is critical. Use this calibration guide:

1 ‚Äî No answer: Couldn't respond at all, or gave a completely wrong answer
2 ‚Äî Weak: Vague, mostly incorrect, shows fundamental gaps
3 ‚Äî Partial: Knows the concept but missed key details or nuances
4 ‚Äî Good: Solid answer with minor gaps, demonstrates clear understanding
5 ‚Äî Perfect: Comprehensive answer, could explain it to others, production-ready knowledge
üí°Calibration tip

Most answers should fall between 2-4. If you're giving mostly 5s, you may be too lenient. If mostly 1-2s, the difficulty level may be too high for the candidate ‚Äî consider adjusting topics.

7. Avoiding Bias

Halo Effect: Don't let one great answer inflate all subsequent ratings
Horn Effect: Don't let one poor answer deflate everything after
Similarity Bias: Don't rate higher because the candidate reminds you of yourself
Anchoring: Rate each question independently ‚Äî don't compare to the last candidate
Confirmation Bias: Don't look for evidence to confirm your first impression
Pressure Discount: Remember that interview pressure affects performance ‚Äî a nervous 4 might be a calm 5

8. Wrap-up & Next Steps

You: "Thanks for your answers! Do you have any questions about the role, the team, or our tech stack?"
You: "Here's what happens next: [explain your hiring timeline]. You should hear back within [X days]."
You: "It was great talking with you. Thanks again for your time!"
üí°Tip

Always let the candidate ask questions. Their questions reveal their priorities and engagement level. This is also valuable signal.

9. Do's & Don'ts

‚úìDo
  • Be warm and encouraging throughout
  • Give the candidate time to think
  • Ask follow-up questions to probe deeper
  • Take notes on specific answers
  • Rate each question immediately
  • Use the full 1-5 scale, not just 3-5
  • Explain the format upfront
  • Let them ask questions at the end
‚úóDon't
  • Interrupt while they're answering
  • Show frustration at wrong answers
  • Ask trick questions or gotchas
  • Compare candidates out loud
  • Skip the introduction phase
  • Let them struggle in silence too long
  • Give hints that reveal the answer
  • Make the interview feel adversarial
